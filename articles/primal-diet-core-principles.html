The Primal Diet: Core Principles

PrimalMail · Issue 02

Strip away the debates and the shock factor, and the Primal Diet — as Aajonus Vonderplanitz taught it — comes down to a small set of rules. Here's the breakdown we come back to whenever someone asks "wait, so what do you actually eat?"

1. Raw meat, not cooked

Beef, lamb, poultry, fish — eaten raw rather than cooked. The core argument: cooking changes the structure of proteins and fats and destroys enzymes the body would otherwise use directly.

Oysters get their own dedicated place in Vonderplanitz's writing, and the reasoning is a lot more specific than "easy entry point." He recommended them for heavy-metal detox — his claim was that oysters clear heavy metals from the body faster than anything else — with a regimen of half to a whole cup of red meat paired with three oysters, twice daily, six days a week with one day off. He was specific about sourcing too: avoid oysters from Florida or the Gulf of Mexico, and favor farmed oysters from open ocean waters instead.

Raw fish more broadly fits the same raw-over-cooked logic as the meat above — we don't have a source pinning down which specific fish he recommended beyond that, so treat any particular fish (salmon included) as a reasonable extension of the framework rather than something he named directly.

2. Raw dairy over pasteurized

Raw milk, raw cream, and raw cheese instead of the pasteurized, homogenized versions on grocery shelves. Pasteurization, in this framework, is a processing step that does more harm than good.

3. Raw eggs

Whole raw eggs, often blended into smoothies or eaten straight — a dense, easy source of raw fat and protein.

4. Raw animal fat as fuel

Fat, not carbohydrate, is treated as the primary energy source — tallow, raw butter, and fat trimmed straight from raw meat. Vonderplanitz was specific about how much: up to 8–16 tablespoons of raw butter a day in his own formulas. The reasoning was that raw fat "cleanses, lubricates, protects and fuels the body easily," while heated or pasteurized fat gets stored as cellulite or hard-to-use waxy fat instead of being used cleanly.

5. Raw fruit — but never alone

Fruit shows up throughout the Primal Diet, just not eaten by itself. Oranges and bananas get blended with raw eggs, raw dairy fat, and unheated honey into smoothies. Strawberries are eaten with raw cream and unheated honey. Raw, unripe pineapple is paired with raw garlic and raw butter. The logic is consistent with the fat rule above: fruit sugar without fat, in this framework, isn't how the body is meant to use it.

Unheated honey specifically gets its own distinction — it can't be warmed above hive temperature (92.8°F), compared to "raw" or "uncooked" honey which can tolerate heating up to 160°F and still carry the label. Vonderplanitz's claim was that 80–90% of truly unheated honey converts into enzymes that aid digestion, which heated honey can't do.

6. Cut the processed and cooked staples

Grains, refined sugar, seed oils, and most cooked food are minimized or removed entirely. If it came out of a factory line or a hot pan, it's not part of this way of eating.

7. Sun, not sunscreen

Regular sun exposure is treated as part of the diet, not separate from it — a rejection of the "avoid the sun at all costs" messaging most people grew up with.

A claim you'll hear a lot in the broader raw/ancestral-health world connects this to rule 6: that polyunsaturated fats from seed oils get incorporated into your skin's fat stores, and being more chemically unstable than the saturated fat in butter or tallow, oxidize more readily under UV exposure — supposedly making skin burn and sun-damage more easily. Worth being upfront that this isn't something we've found in Vonderplanitz's own writing specifically — it's a claim from the wider seed-oil-skeptic community, not confirmed as part of his original framework, so treat it as a related idea rather than his doctrine. Either way, the sun itself isn't treated as the problem here — what you're eating alongside it is.

The through-line across all seven rules: eat foods as close as possible to their raw, unprocessed state, and be skeptical of anything modern food science tells you to do instead.

This is the framework that shows up, in one form or another, across almost every raw-meat and anti-vegan creator working today — including the movement Goatis has built. Next issue, we're getting into that.
